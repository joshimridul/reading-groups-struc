\author{Mridul Joshi}

\begin{abstract}\noindent
When within-grade achievement variance is wide, schools can use a diagnostic assessment to reassign students across adjacent grades into ability-based instructional tracks. This cross-grade grouping can sharpen instructional targeting, but it also reorganizes classrooms---changing their size, peer composition, and age-grade structure. We study two randomized evaluations of cross-grade reading groups in Kenya and Liberia. Neither produces a positive average effect, but for different reasons. In Liberia, the baseline assessment explains less than 7\% of endline variation; regrouping based on it does reduce average mismatch to track targets, but the assignment gains are modest and are swamped by the costs of reorganization---class sizes increase by a third. In Kenya, the baseline explains over 50\% and regrouping substantially compresses within-class achievement dispersion, yet mean scores do not rise. Exploiting the fact that scripted curricula fix instructional content by track, we find evidence that adverse changes in peer composition offset the targeting gains. The contrast reveals a general tension in assignment under imperfect information: a diagnostic that improves on the incumbent rule along one dimension can worsen outcomes if acting on it disrupts the organizational structure---class sizes, peer groups, age cohorts---in which instruction takes place.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Schools must assign heterogeneous students to a limited number of instructional environments. The standard rule is grade, which groups students by age and prior progression through the curriculum. Grade is simple to administer, and it carries useful information about curricular exposure and developmental stage. Yet grade can be poorly aligned with actual ability when students within the same grade differ sharply in achievement and students in adjacent grades overlap substantially in skill.

The mismatch between grade and ability is particularly severe in many developing-country school systems. Within-grade achievement variance is wide, automatic promotion policies leave students far behind grade-level expectations, and multi-grade classrooms are common in small or under-resourced schools \citep{banerjee/cole/duflo/linden:2007,muralidharan2019disrupting}. In these settings, a single teacher may face a classroom in which some students cannot recognize letters while others read fluently---a range that makes grade-level instruction poorly suited to most of the class. Cross-grade ability grouping is an appealing response. It requires no new teachers, materials, or technology---only a reassignment of existing students to existing classrooms based on a diagnostic assessment. And in schools with a single class per grade, within-grade tracking is not feasible: there are too few students per grade to form separate instructional groups without pooling across grades. Cross-grade grouping is therefore the natural organizational form when schools want to teach at assessed level rather than at enrolled grade. Recent evidence that aligning instruction to students' demonstrated level can improve learning reinforces the case for trying \citep{banerjee2017proof,duflo2020experimental}.

The underlying problem, though, is one of assignment under noisy information. Grade-based assignment and score-based assignment are both imperfect rules for instructional placement. Grade is coarse on measured achievement; a diagnostic score captures current performance more directly but may omit other determinants of successful placement---age, curricular exposure, classroom manageability. And regrouping changes the classroom itself: it alters peer composition, can increase or decrease class sizes, and may affect students' relative standing within the group \citep{betts:2011}. A reclassification that improves targeting on paper may still fail if the resulting classrooms become less productive.

We study this question using two randomized evaluations of cross-grade reading groups in schools operated by NewGlobe Education, a large school network that uses scripted lesson plans delivered via digital tablets. In 2018, approximately 90 Liberian public schools managed by NewGlobe were randomized to regroup students in Grades 1--4 into two reading tracks based on a baseline reading assessment. In the same year, 50 out of 300 NewGlobe academies in Kenya were randomized to regroup students in Grades 1--2 into two reading groups based on a combined literacy and English test. In both cases, regrouping applied to reading instruction only: students were reassigned to cross-grade reading classrooms for a daily instructional block, then returned to their enrolled grade for all other subjects. Because NewGlobe uses scripted curricula, instructional level in each track was determined by the track designation rather than by teacher discretion. This design is more demanding than within-grade tracking, which splits an existing grade cohort while preserving its age-grade structure. Cross-grade grouping uses the diagnostic rather than grade as the organizing rule for instruction---a change that can correct more severe instructional mismatch when learning levels overlap across grades, but that also removes students from their age cohort and reorganizes classroom composition more fundamentally.

Neither experiment produces a positive average treatment effect. The Liberia estimate is negative ($-0.212$ SD, $p = 0.12$) and the Kenya estimate is a precisely estimated zero ($0.034$ SD, $p = 0.55$), ruling out effects as large as the $0.18$ SD found by \citet{duflo2011peer} for within-grade tracking. The two experiments differ sharply in the quality of the diagnostic signal and the severity of classroom reorganization, and the comparison reveals why each null arises.

In Liberia, the baseline assessment is a very weak predictor of endline achievement, explaining less than 7\% of endline variation. Regrouping based on it does reduce average mismatch between students and track instructional targets, but it barely compresses within-class achievement dispersion---the sorted classrooms remain nearly as heterogeneous as the unsorted ones. Meanwhile, regrouping increases class sizes by roughly 13 students. The harm falls on the upper track ($-0.348$ SD, $p = 0.03$) and extends across the ability distribution rather than clustering near the assignment cutoff---a pattern more consistent with a deterioration of the upper-track classroom as a whole than with marginal misclassification of students near the threshold. Treated schools follow deterministic score cutoffs with zero observed assignment error; the modest assignment gains are real, but they are swamped by the costs of classroom reorganization.

In Kenya, the baseline is far more predictive, explaining over 50\% of endline variation, and regrouping does compress within-class dispersion. Yet mean achievement does not rise. The Kenya setting allows us to ask why. Because scripted curricula fix instructional content by track, two students with the same baseline score assigned to the same track receive the same instruction regardless of who else is in their classroom. Under scripted curricula, instructional content is fixed by track designation, so conditioning on baseline-score bin and treatment status approximately absorbs the instructional-targeting channel. This creates leverage to examine the peer-composition channel in isolation---a distinction that is normally confounded in tracking studies. We find that higher peer ability \emph{reduces} own achievement, consistent with students performing worse when regrouping lowers their standing relative to classmates, though the estimate captures a composite of peer-level and rank effects that we cannot fully separate. The sorting rule works as intended in Kenya; the problem is that the classrooms it creates are less productive than the ones it replaces.

A simple model of score-based grouping organizes these findings. It decomposes the effect of replacing grade-based with score-based assignment into four channels---assignment gains from reducing instructional mismatch, changes in productive instructional time, peer-composition effects, and relative-rank effects---and delivers a threshold condition: regrouping raises achievement only when the informational gain from the diagnostic exceeds the costs of classroom reorganization.

The paper makes three contributions. First, we provide the first experimental evidence on cross-grade ability grouping---what the early American literature called the Joplin Plan \citep{floyd:1954}.\footnote{There is also a large body of research on between-school ability grouping, mainly from Europe where tracking between academic and vocational tracks is prevalent \citep{fu2018ability,lavrijsen2015new}. We are interested in within-school ability grouping.} Most meta-analyses of within-grade tracking find small or null average effects \citep{slavin:1987,slavin1990achievement,kulik1987effects,terrin2022effect}, with heterogeneous results across tracks \citep{burke2013classroom,hoxby2005taking,antecol2016peer,garlick,de2022college}. \citet{slavin:1987} judged the early Joplin Plan studies promising, but they were small and none used random assignment. In developing countries, well-identified evidence is scarce. The benchmark study is \citet{duflo2011peer}, who randomly assigned Kenyan primary schools to track first-graders into two within-grade sections and found average gains of $0.18$ SD, with effects across the baseline ability distribution. Cross-grade grouping is a more demanding design. As we show in Section~\ref{sec:theory} (Remark~\ref{rem:wg_cg}), within-grade tracking holds grade fixed and asks the diagnostic only to rank students within a single grade's ability distribution; it preserves age-grade structure and keeps within-classroom grade dispersion at zero by construction. Cross-grade grouping pools students across grades and requires the diagnostic to produce commensurable rankings across grade-specific distributions that may be shrunk at very different rates. It also mechanically increases grade mixing and can substantially change class sizes. Our experiments show that these additional costs can dominate even when the sorting rule succeeds on its own terms.

Second, the two-experiment design allows us to explain \emph{why} ability grouping fails in each setting, not just report that it does. The tracking literature has documented considerable heterogeneity in effects across contexts \citep{betts:2011,card2016can,vardardottir2013peer} but has had difficulty explaining its sources because each study confounds multiple channels---instructional targeting, peer composition, class size, relative standing---that change simultaneously when students are regrouped. Our two experiments share the same institutional structure and implementation protocol but differ sharply in signal quality and classroom reallocation. In Kenya, because scripted curricula fix instructional content by track, conditioning on baseline-score bin and treatment status approximately absorbs the instructional-targeting channel, creating leverage to examine peer-composition effects---a distinction that is normally confounded. Tracking also changes classroom dispersion, an input that studies of classroom production often overlook in favor of mean peer achievement \citep{rjosk2022dispersion,vigdor2007peer,duflo2011peer}.

Third, we develop a framework for evaluating assignment rules that goes beyond the question of whether a diagnostic predicts achievement. The Teaching at the Right Level literature asks whether instruction targeted to assessed level raises scores \citep{banerjee2017proof,duflo2020experimental}; proponents of tracking argue that reducing within-class dispersion allows teachers to target instruction more effectively \citep{duflo2011peer}. Our framework asks a different question: whether a score-based assignment rule improves on the incumbent rule---grade---after accounting for the organizational costs of acting on it. Tracking changes not only the level of instruction but also the composition and size of the classroom, and the literature on mixed-grade classrooms suggests that the sign of these effects is context-dependent \citep{sims2008strategic,leuven2011classroom}. The model delivers a threshold condition that depends jointly on signal quality and classroom reorganization, and the two experiments land on opposite sides of the margins that matter.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:theory} develops the theoretical framework. Section~\ref{sec:design} describes the experiments and data. Section~\ref{sec:strategy} presents the empirical strategy. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results. Section~\ref{sec:robustness} discusses robustness. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:theory}
%% ============================================================

We model cross-grade ability grouping as a classification problem under a noisy signal. Students differ in latent ability, but schools observe only an imperfect diagnostic. A score-based reassignment rule can improve achievement by placing students in classrooms better matched to their level, but it also reorganizes the classroom: changing class sizes, mixing students from different developmental stages, shifting peer composition, and altering students' relative standing.

\subsection{Environment and Assignment}

Student $i$ in grade $g(i) \in \mathcal{G}$ has latent ability
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ability}
\theta_i = \mu_{g(i)} + \nu_i,
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{g(i)}$ is the grade-specific mean and $\nu_i$ captures within-grade heterogeneity, with $\E[\nu_i] = 0$ and $\text{Var}(\nu_i \mid g(i)) = \sigma^2_{\nu,g}$. The school does not observe $\theta_i$ directly. It observes a noisy diagnostic score
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:score}
s_i = \theta_i + u_i,
\end{equation}
with $\E[u_i \mid \theta_i, g(i)] = 0$ and $\text{Var}(u_i \mid g(i)) = \sigma^2_{u,g}$. The school's problem is analogous to an employer deciding whether to use a test score in addition to an observable credential (here, grade) when assigning workers to tasks \citep{AutorScarborough2008}. Grade already provides some information about ability; the diagnostic is valuable only if it moves the school meaningfully beyond what grade already reveals. When the test is noisy relative to the within-grade ability distribution, the posterior $\E[\theta_i \mid s_i, g(i)]$ is pulled back toward the grade mean, and reassignment based on the score is little better than leaving students in their enrolled grade.

The posterior expectation of ability given the signal and grade is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:posterior}
a_i \equiv \E[\theta_i \mid s_i, g(i)] = \mu_{g(i)} + \rho_g(s_i - \mu_{g(i)}),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:signal_quality}
\rho_g \equiv \frac{\sigma^2_{\nu,g}}{\sigma^2_{\nu,g} + \sigma^2_{u,g}}
\end{equation}
is the signal quality of the diagnostic. When $\rho_g$ is high, the posterior tracks the raw score closely and the diagnostic carries substantial information beyond grade; when $\rho_g$ is low, the posterior is pulled toward the grade mean and reassignment based on the test is only a weak improvement over grade assignment. The parameter $\rho_g$ is not directly observed. Our empirical proxy is $R^2_g$, the within-grade $R^2$ from regressing endline scores on baseline scores in the control group. Under the model's Gaussian structure, $R^2_g = \rho_g \cdot \sigma^2_{\nu,g}/(\sigma^2_{\nu,g} + \sigma^2_{\varepsilon,g})$, where $\sigma^2_{\varepsilon,g}$ is endline noise; because endline noise attenuates the correlation, $R^2_g < \rho_g$, so the empirical measure is a conservative proxy for the model's shrinkage weight. We refer to $R^2_g$ as \emph{baseline predictive power}. It blends test precision, achievement persistence, and baseline-endline construct alignment into a single measure of how useful the diagnostic is for assignment---whatever the source of a low $R^2_g$, a signal with low predictive power provides less useful information for reassignment.

Under the status quo regime ($G$), students remain in grade-based classrooms and receive grade-level instruction $I_i^G = I(g(i))$. Under score-based grouping ($T$), the school assigns students to a low or high instructional track using the posterior signal:
\begin{equation}
k_i^T =
\begin{cases}
L & \text{if } a_i < c, \\
H & \text{if } a_i \geq c,
\end{cases}
\qquad
I_i^T =
\begin{cases}
I_L & \text{if } k_i^T = L, \\
I_H & \text{if } k_i^T = H.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Under scripted curricula---where lesson plans are standardized and teacher discretion over content is limited---instructional level is fixed by track designation rather than adjusted by the teacher. This means that the instructional content a student receives depends only on which track she is assigned to, not on who else ends up in her classroom. Both experiments we study operate under scripted instruction (Section~\ref{sec:design}). The assignment decision---high or low track---and the classroom-reorganization consequences---peer quality, class size---are therefore institutionally distinct, even though they are jointly determined by the same score rule.

\subsection{Achievement}

Endline achievement under assignment regime $a \in \{G, T\}$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:learning}
Y_i(a) = \theta_i + \tau_{c(i,a)} \left[\alpha - \lambda\big(\theta_i - I_i(a)\big)^2 + \pi\bar{\theta}_{-i,c(i,a)} + \eta\, r_{i,c(i,a)}\right] + \varepsilon_i(a).
\end{equation}
Baseline ability $\theta_i$ persists into the endline: a student who learns nothing during the intervention still retains her initial achievement level. The bracketed term is the value added by instruction, which depends on how well instruction is matched to the student's level, who her classmates are, and how much classroom time is productively used. This term is scaled by $\tau_c$, so all instructional channels are attenuated when the classroom becomes harder to manage.

The term $(\theta_i - I_i(a))^2$ is \emph{instructional mismatch}: a student whose ability is far from the instructional target of her classroom learns less than one whose ability is close to it, regardless of whether the instruction is too advanced or too basic. The quadratic form implies that the cost of mismatch is convex---small deviations matter little, but large deviations are increasingly costly. Under grade-based assignment, mismatch arises because students within the same grade differ in ability but receive the same instruction. Under score-based grouping, the diagnostic can reduce mismatch by sorting students into tracks whose instructional targets are closer to their ability, but only if the diagnostic is informative enough for the sorting to be accurate.

The term $\bar{\theta}_{-i,c(i,a)}$ captures the level of peer ability in student $i$'s classroom, while $r_{i,c(i,a)} \equiv \text{rank}_i / N_{c(i,a)}$ denotes student $i$'s percentile rank within that classroom. We normalize by class size so that rank is comparable across classrooms of different sizes---an important consideration here, since tracking changes class sizes. Both terms are needed because regrouping can place a student among stronger peers while simultaneously lowering that student's relative standing, so the net social effect of tracking is not signed \emph{a priori}. The sign of $\pi$ is theoretically ambiguous---positive under peer learning, negative if lower-ability peers generate disruption. The coefficient $\eta$ captures relative-standing effects: percentile rank affects achievement independently of the level of surrounding peers \citep{murphy2020top, elsner2021rank}, consistent with the ``big-fish-little-pond'' effect in educational psychology \citep{hoxby2005taking}.

The object $\tau_{c(i,a)} \in (0, 1]$ is the fraction of classroom time that is productively engaged. Following the logic of \citet{Lazear2001}, productive time is a classroom-level public good: when the classroom becomes harder to manage, all students lose instructional time. We let
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tau}
\tau_c = \tau\!\big(N_c,\, V^g_c\big), \qquad \frac{\partial\tau}{\partial N_c} < 0, \quad \frac{\partial\tau}{\partial V^g_c} < 0,
\end{equation}
where $N_c$ is class size and $V^g_c \equiv \text{Var}_{i \in c}(g(i))$ is within-classroom grade dispersion. The class-size channel is standard: larger classrooms reduce the share of time available per student. The grade-dispersion channel is specific to cross-grade grouping. A diagnostic sorts students on one measured dimension (reading level), but students from different grades differ on unmeasured dimensions---behavioral maturity, attention span, familiarity with classroom routines---that affect how easily the classroom functions as a unit. When regrouping pools adjacent grades, each student's probability of disrupting is increasing in the distance between that student's developmental stage and the classroom's modal stage; the aggregate disruption rate is therefore increasing in within-class grade dispersion, which rises mechanically in treatment classrooms.

Because $\tau_c$ multiplies the entire bracketed term, a fall in productive time attenuates all other channels: if regrouping creates a classroom that is too large or too heterogeneous in developmental stage for the teacher to manage, the returns to better instructional match, stronger peers, or higher rank are all diminished. Correct placement does not help much in a classroom where effective instruction is not occurring.

\subsection{Treatment Effect Decomposition}

Define student $i$'s \emph{assignment gain} as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Bi}
B_i \equiv \big(\theta_i - I_i^G\big)^2 - \big(\theta_i - I_i^T\big)^2.
\end{equation}
A positive $B_i$ means that score-based grouping moves student $i$ closer to the instructional level that fits her ability. Let
\begin{equation}
A_i(a) \equiv \alpha - \lambda\big(\theta_i - I_i(a)\big)^2 + \pi\bar{\theta}_{-i,c(i,a)} + \eta\, r_{i,c(i,a)}
\end{equation}
collect the within-brackets terms. The treatment effect for student $i$ separates into two parts: changes in the quality of instruction holding productive time fixed, and changes in productive time holding instruction quality at its treatment level:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:decomp}
\Delta Y_i \equiv Y_i(T) - Y_i(G) = \tau_{c(i,G)}\big[A_i(T) - A_i(G)\big] + \big[\tau_{c(i,T)} - \tau_{c(i,G)}\big] A_i(T) + \tilde{\varepsilon}_i,
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\varepsilon}_i = \varepsilon_i(T) - \varepsilon_i(G)$. Expanding gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:decomp_full}
\Delta Y_i = \underbrace{\lambda\tau_{c(i,G)} B_i}_{\text{assignment gain}} + \underbrace{\pi\tau_{c(i,G)}\Delta\bar{\theta}_i}_{\text{peer level}} + \underbrace{\eta\tau_{c(i,G)}\Delta r_i}_{\text{rank}} + \underbrace{\big[\tau_{c(i,T)} - \tau_{c(i,G)}\big] A_i(T)}_{\text{productive time}} + \tilde{\varepsilon}_i,
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\bar{\theta}_i \equiv \bar{\theta}_{-i,c(i,T)} - \bar{\theta}_{-i,c(i,G)}$ and $\Delta r_i \equiv r_{i,c(i,T)} - r_{i,c(i,G)}$.

Score-based grouping therefore affects achievement through four channels: it can improve instructional match ($B_i$), change peer composition ($\Delta\bar{\theta}_i$), change relative rank ($\Delta r_i$), and alter the amount of productive instructional time available ($\Delta\tau_c$). Averaging over students yields
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ate}
\text{ATE} = \lambda\,\E[\tau_{c(i,G)} B_i] + \pi\,\E[\tau_{c(i,G)}\Delta\bar{\theta}_i] + \eta\,\E[\tau_{c(i,G)}\Delta r_i] + \E\!\big[(\tau_{c(i,T)} - \tau_{c(i,G)})A_i(T)\big].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Signal Quality and the Value of Grouping}

Under the Gaussian structure, the posterior $a_i$ concentrates around $\theta_i$ as $\rho_g$ rises, so track assignments become more accurate and the reduction in mismatch grows.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:threshold}
Under Gaussian $\nu_i$ and $u_i$ with a monotone cutoff rule:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] The expected assignment gain $\E[B_i \mid \rho_g]$ is strictly increasing in $\rho_g$ on $(0,1)$, with $\E[B_i \mid \rho_g = 0] = 0$ and $\E[B_i \mid \rho_g = 1] = \E\!\big[(\theta_i - I_i^G)^2 - (\theta_i - I_i^{T*})^2\big]$, where $I_i^{T*}$ is the oracle-optimal track target under perfect observation of $\theta_i$.
\item[(ii)] If $\text{ATE}(\rho_g = 1) > 0$ and $\text{ATE}(\rho_g)$ is continuous in $\rho_g$, then since $\text{ATE}(0) \leq 0$, there exists at least one $\rho^*_g \in (0,1)$ such that $\text{ATE}(\rho^*_g) = 0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\noindent \emph{Proof sketch.} Part (i): When $\rho_g = 0$, the posterior $a_i = \mu_{g(i)}$ for all students in the same grade: the test provides no information beyond grade, so track assignment is determined entirely by grade and $\E[B_i] = 0$. As $\rho_g$ rises, the posterior concentrates around $\theta_i$, a strictly larger fraction of students are assigned to the track that minimizes their mismatch, and $\E[B_i]$ rises continuously. At $\rho_g = 1$, the posterior equals true ability and assignment is oracle-optimal given the available tracks, yielding the maximal mismatch reduction. Since $\tau_{c(i,G)}$ does not depend on $\rho_g$ (it is determined by the status quo classroom), $\E[\tau_{c(i,G)} B_i]$ inherits the monotonicity. Part (ii): At $\rho_g = 0$, the assignment-gain term is zero while the reorganization terms---peer composition, rank, productive time---can be negative (regrouping still moves students across classrooms without informational gain). So $\text{ATE}(0) \leq 0$. If $\text{ATE}(1) > 0$, continuity gives at least one crossing. \qed

The reorganization terms---$\E[\tau_{c(i,G)}\Delta\bar{\theta}_i]$, $\E[\tau_{c(i,G)}\Delta r_i]$, and $\E[(\tau_T - \tau_G)A_i(T)]$---are themselves functions of $\rho_g$ through the assignment, so the threshold need not be unique. When the signal is poor, near-random reassignment simultaneously fails to reduce mismatch, scrambles peer composition, and---in settings where regrouping increases class sizes or grade mixing---reduces productive time. All four terms in equation~\eqref{eq:ate} can be negative simultaneously. The production function parameters ($\lambda$, $\pi$, $\eta$, $\tau_c$) need not be the same across settings. The model asks whether the assignment gain outweighs the reorganization costs; the answer can differ across settings because signal quality, class sizes, and composition shifts differ.

\begin{remark}[Within-grade vs.\ cross-grade tracking]
\label{rem:wg_cg}
The decomposition in equation~\eqref{eq:ate} applies to any score-based reassignment, but the magnitudes of the terms differ systematically between within-grade tracking (splitting a single grade into tracks) and cross-grade tracking (pooling adjacent grades and re-sorting).

\emph{Productive time.} Under within-grade tracking, all students in a classroom share the same grade, so $V^g_c = 0$ by construction. Under cross-grade tracking, treatment classrooms pool students from different grades, so $V^g_c > 0$. Since $\partial\tau/\partial V^g_c < 0$, cross-grade tracking incurs a productive-time cost that within-grade tracking avoids entirely. Class sizes also differ: within-grade tracking splits an existing grade-level classroom roughly in half ($N^{WG}_c \approx N_g/2$), while cross-grade tracking determines class sizes by the ability distribution across the pooled grades, which can substantially increase $N_c$.

\emph{Assignment gain.} Under within-grade tracking with a grade-specific cutoff $c_g$, student $i$'s assignment depends on $a_i - c_g = \rho_{g(i)}(s_i - \mu_{g(i)}) - (c_g - \mu_{g(i)})$, which involves only the signal quality $\rho_{g(i)}$ of her own grade. Under cross-grade tracking with a common cutoff $c$, student $i$'s assignment depends on
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cg_assign}
a_i - c = \mu_{g(i)} + \rho_{g(i)}(s_i - \mu_{g(i)}) - c.
\end{equation}
Whether student $i$ in grade $g$ is assigned above or below a student $j$ in grade $g'$ now depends on both $\rho_g$ and $\rho_{g'}$. When signal quality varies across grades, posteriors are shrunk at different rates: a student in a high-$\rho$ grade is ranked largely on ability, while a student in a low-$\rho$ grade is ranked largely on her grade mean. The cross-grade ranking conflates these differently-informative signals, distorting assignment in a way that within-grade tracking does not face.
\end{remark}

\section{Experimental Design and Data}
\label{sec:design}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Setting}

Both experiments take place in schools operated by NewGlobe Education. NewGlobe operates large networks of low-cost schools using a standardized model: scripted lesson plans delivered through digital tablets, with teacher compliance monitored by school managers. Because lesson content is scripted, instructional level is determined by track designation rather than by teacher discretion---a feature that plays a central role in the peer-effects identification strategy (Section~\ref{sec:bh}).

\subsection{Experiment 1: Kenya (2018)}

In August 2018, 50 out of approximately 300 NewGlobe academies in Kenya were randomized to receive the reading group intervention. In treated schools, students in Grades 1 and 2 were assigned to one of two reading groups (``Blue'' and ``Purple'') based on a combined literacy and English baseline test administered in June 2018. Grade 1 students scoring above 40 points were assigned to the Purple (upper) group; Grade 2 students above 35 points. Treatment was stratified by constituency. Students received 90 minutes of instruction with their reading group daily for Term 3 (approximately 3 months).

\subsection{Experiment 2: Liberia (2018)}

Approximately 90 public schools in Liberia, managed by NewGlobe under the Liberia Education Advancement Program, were randomized into treatment and control. In treatment schools, students in Grades 1--4 were regrouped: Grades 1 and 2 were combined and split into two tracks, as were Grades 3 and 4. The diagnostic test was a baseline reading assessment; the cutoff for the upper track was 23 points for the Grade 1--2 pool and 14 points for the Grade 3--4 pool. Treatment was stratified by grade group and treatment probability.

\subsection{Data}

Baseline data consist of literacy and English language test scores administered before the intervention in each experiment. Endline data consist of literacy assessments administered at the end of the school year. In Kenya, students in different grades took grade-specific tests; we standardize all scores to have mean zero and unit standard deviation in the control group within each grade. Tables~\ref{tab:lib_sampleflow} and~\ref{tab:ke_sampleflow} in the appendix document the sample construction.

Table~\ref{tab:lib_sumstats} reports summary statistics for the Liberia sample, and Table~\ref{tab:ke_sumstats} reports summary statistics for Kenya. Figure~\ref{fig:bl_dist} plots the baseline score distributions by grade and treatment status; the substantial overlap across grades confirms the scope for cross-grade regrouping.

\subsection{Balance and Attrition}

Table~\ref{tab:balance_1do} reports covariate balance across treatment and control schools. Pre-treatment characteristics---baseline scores, grade composition, and school size---are jointly balanced in both experiments, consistent with successful randomization.

Table~\ref{tab:attrition} reports attrition rates by treatment status, experiment, and baseline-ability quartile. Overall attrition is balanced in Liberia. In Kenya, attrition is nontrivially lower in treated schools (8.5 percentage points), though it does not vary systematically with baseline ability. To bound the potential impact of differential attrition, Table~\ref{tab:lee_bounds} reports \citet{lee2009training} trimming bounds. The Kenya Lee bounds shift the ITT from $0.034$ to a range of $-0.019$ to $0.152$; the upper bound still rules out effects as large as the $0.18$ SD found by \citet{duflo2011peer}. In Liberia, the bounds do not materially change the inference. Selective attrition is therefore a real concern in Kenya, but the bounded range does not change the qualitative conclusion that cross-grade grouping fails to produce the large positive effects found in within-grade tracking studies.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:strategy}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Intent-to-Treat Effects}

We estimate the overall treatment effect using:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:itt}
Y_i = \alpha + \beta T_j + \gamma \hat{\theta}^{EB}_i + \phi_s + \varepsilon_i
\end{equation}
where $Y_i$ is the standardized endline score, $T_j$ is school-level treatment assignment, $\phi_s$ are strata fixed effects, and standard errors are clustered at the school-grade-group level. Because treatment was randomized at the school level, OLS identifies the average intent-to-treat effect $\beta$. We also estimate effects by grade group and by track assignment (upper vs.\ lower), interacting treatment with the relevant indicators.

The baseline control $\hat{\theta}^{EB}_i$ is an empirical Bayes ability measure that shrinks each student's raw score toward the grade mean by the grade-specific signal quality:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eb}
\hat{\theta}^{EB}_{i} = \bar{s}_{g(i)} + \hat{\rho}_{g(i)}\,(s_i - \bar{s}_{g(i)}),
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\rho}_{g(i)} \equiv \sqrt{R^2_{g(i)}}$ and $R^2_g$ is the within-grade baseline-endline $R^2$ in the control group (Section~\ref{sec:theory}). Because $R^2_g < \rho_g$, the EB measure shrinks more aggressively than the model's posterior, making the resulting ability estimates conservative.\footnote{When $\hat{\rho}_g$ is constant across grades, $\hat{\theta}^{EB}$ is an affine transformation of $s_i$ and the mismatch measure is a rescaled version of raw-score misfit. Grade-specific shrinkage matters here because $R^2_g$ ranges from $\approx 0.03$ (Liberia Grade 3) to $\approx 0.56$ (Kenya Grade 2). In the ITT specification, $\hat{\theta}^{EB}_i$ enters linearly as a baseline covariate; with grade fixed effects already in the model, grade-specific shrinkage is equivalent to interacting baseline scores with grade.} Beyond its role as a baseline control, $\hat{\theta}^{EB}$ is used to construct the mismatch measure $\text{Misfit}_i = (\hat{\theta}^{EB}_i - \widehat{I}_{k(i)})^2$, where $\widehat{I}_{k(i)}$ is the estimated target ability level of track $k(i)$. Grade-specific shrinkage ensures that misfit downweights score variation in grades where the test is uninformative, so that the measure reflects genuine ability dispersion rather than measurement error.

\subsection{Effect on Within-Class Dispersion}

Before asking whether tracking raises achievement, we ask a prior question: does it actually compress within-class ability heterogeneity? The answer is not obvious. Tracking sorts students on a noisy signal, so the realized compression depends on how informative the diagnostic is. If signal quality is low, the reassignment is close to random and within-class dispersion falls little.

We measure dispersion using baseline scores rather than endline scores. Baseline scores are pre-determined, so within-class baseline dispersion is a purely mechanical function of who the sorting rule places together---it is not affected by any treatment-induced changes in achievement. Using endline scores would conflate the sorting effect with treatment effects on the distribution of outcomes. We estimate:
\begin{equation}
|s_i - \bar{s}_{k(i)}| = \alpha + \beta T_j + \gamma \hat{\theta}^{EB}_i + \phi_s + \varepsilon_i
\end{equation}
where $|s_i - \bar{s}_{k(i)}|$ is student $i$'s absolute deviation from the mean baseline score in her reading classroom $k(i)$. A negative $\beta$ indicates that tracking compresses within-class heterogeneity. Figure~\ref{fig:dispersion} plots the classroom-level standard deviation of baseline scores in treatment and control classrooms directly.

\subsection{Isolating Peer Effects}
\label{sec:bh}

When a school is randomized into tracking, everything about the classroom changes at once: peer composition, instructional match, class size, grade mixing. A naive regression of outcomes on peer ability conflates the direct effect of peers with all of these correlated changes. To make progress, we need variation in peer composition that is not driven by variation in other classroom characteristics.

The experiment provides such variation. Consider two students in different treated schools who have the same baseline score and are therefore assigned to the same track. Their instructional targets are identical, but their classmates differ because the composition of each school differs. One student may end up in a reading group where the other students in her track happen to be relatively strong; another may end up with weaker peers. This variation in peer composition is not chosen by the student or the school---it is a mechanical consequence of which other students happen to attend the same school, combined with the exogenous treatment assignment that determines whether and how students are regrouped.

The problem is that students' \emph{exposure} to this peer composition is nonrandom. Let $P_i = (1/n_k) \sum_{j \neq i} s_j$ denote the mean baseline score of student $i$'s classmates. $P_i$ is what \citet{borusyak2023nonrandom} call a \emph{formula} regressor: a known, deterministic function of the treatment assignment $T_j$ and the vector of predetermined baseline scores in the school.\footnote{The connection to BH is as follows. In BH's framework, a formula regressor is a known function of exogenous shocks and pre-determined exposure shares, and identification is restored by purging its expected value under the known shock-assignment process. Here, leave-out peer mean $P_i$ is likewise a formula object, but it depends on both the randomized treatment status $T_j$ and the realized composition of the school. Because treatment is randomized and track assignment follows deterministic cutoffs, the BH recentering step is feasible in principle: for each student, one can compute the expected peer mean by averaging $P_i$ over the treatment lottery conditional on the school's observed score distribution. What BH's recentering handles is the bias from nonrandom \emph{exposure} to the treatment shock---the fact that a student's own score determines which track she enters and therefore which peers she faces. What it does not provide is the peer-effects exclusion restriction needed to interpret the residualized coefficient as a causal social effect: that requires our Assumption~2, which states that cross-school variation in classmate composition is uninformative about own unobservables conditional on own score. This is a standard peer-effects identifying assumption, not something that follows from BH's shock-exogeneity condition. We implement the recentering step approximately, using score-bin fixed effects as a control-function rather than computing exact conditional expectations (see below).} Although the treatment assignment is random, a student's realized peer mean depends on her own score---higher-scoring students in treated schools are sorted into the upper track and thereby exposed to different peers---so $P_i$ is correlated with own ability and hence with unobserved determinants of achievement. BH's insight is that this bias can be removed by residualizing $P_i$ with respect to its expected value conditional on the student's own characteristics:
\begin{equation}
\mu_P(s_i, T_j, g_i) = \E[P_i \mid s_i, T_j, g_i].
\end{equation}
Once we condition on this expectation, the remaining variation in $P_i$---the deviation $\tilde{P}_i = P_i - \mu_P(s_i, T_j, g_i)$---reflects only the idiosyncratic composition of the particular school, not the student's own characteristics or treatment status.

Three assumptions underpin this strategy:

\begin{assumption}[Random Assignment]
$T_j \perp (\theta_i, u_i, \varepsilon_i)$ for all $i \in j$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Conditional Independence]
$\{s_k\}_{k \in j, k \neq i} \perp (\theta_i, \varepsilon_i) \mid s_i, T_j$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Scripted Instruction]
The instructional level $I_{kg}$ in each track $k$ is a deterministic function of the track designation and is not adjusted by the teacher in response to realized classroom composition.
\end{assumption}

\noindent Assumption~1 holds by design. Assumption~2 is a peer-effects exclusion restriction: conditional on a student's own score and treatment status, the scores of her schoolmates are uninformative about her unobserved determinants of achievement. This is related to, but distinct from, BH's shock-exogeneity condition: BH consider settings where an exogenous shock (here, treatment assignment) generates a formula regressor with nonrandom exposure; our Assumption~2 additionally requires that classmate scores carry no information about own unobservables beyond what own score already captures. Under these two assumptions, $\E[\tilde{P}_i \cdot \varepsilon_i \mid s_i, T_j] = 0$.

We implement this using a control-function approach in the spirit of BH. Rather than computing $\mu_P(s_i, T_j, g_i)$ exactly and recentering $P_i$ by it, we include baseline-decile $\times$ treatment $\times$ grade fixed effects that approximate this conditional expectation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bh}
Y_i = \zeta P_i + \sum_{d,t,g} \delta_{dtg} \cdot \ind\{D_i = d, T_j = t, G_i = g\} + \phi_s + \varepsilon_i.
\end{equation}
The decile$\times$treatment$\times$grade cells absorb the expected peer composition for each student type up to within-cell variation in $s_i$, so $\zeta$ is identified from deviations of actual peer composition around that cell-level expectation. Grade is included in the conditioning because the baseline score distribution differs across grades, so two students in the same decile but different grades face different expected peer compositions. BH show that this control-function approach is valid when the fixed effects linearly span the conditional expectation $\mu_P$; in practice, the discretization is approximate, so we probe sensitivity to finer bins (ventiles) in Section~\ref{sec:robustness}. The stability of $\hat{\zeta}$ across decile and ventile specifications suggests the approximation is adequate.

Assumption~3 plays a distinct role in separating peer composition from instructional targeting. In most tracking settings, instructional match and peer composition move together and cannot be separated: a student placed in a higher track gets both better-matched instruction and different peers. With scripted curricula, instructional level is fixed by track designation. The mismatch measure $\hat{M}_i = (s_i - I_{D_i})^2$ is therefore approximately a deterministic function of $(s_i, T_j)$: it is absorbed by decile$\times$treatment fixed effects up to within-decile score variation. This approximately breaks the collinearity between peer composition and instructional match, so that $\zeta$ captures the peer-composition effect net of mismatch to the extent that the discretization is fine enough.


\subsection{Accounting Decomposition}
\label{sec:accounting}

The model decomposes the ATE into four terms (equation~\ref{eq:ate}): assignment gain, peer composition, relative rank, and productive time. Of these, only the peer/rank composite can be credibly estimated from the data. The mismatch penalty $\lambda$ is not separately identified: misfit is a quadratic function of ability, so it is collinear with flexible ability controls in any regression that includes them. The productive-time function $\tau(N_c, V^g_c)$ cannot be estimated from the treatment-induced variation in class size and grade mixing without strong functional-form assumptions. In Kenya, however, administrative records of lesson completion provide a direct, reduced-form measure of productive instructional time that does not require estimating the structural parameters (Section~\ref{sec:results}). Rather than imposing functional-form assumptions on the remaining channels, we pursue a simpler accounting exercise that requires only one estimated parameter.

The recentered regression in Section~\ref{sec:bh} estimates $\hat{\zeta}$, the reduced-form effect of a one-SD increase in leave-out peer mean on own achievement. Because peer mean and within-class rank move together in tracked classrooms---when classmates get stronger, own rank falls---$\hat{\zeta}$ captures a composite of peer-level and rank effects operating through the peer-mean shift.\footnote{The model decomposes the social channel into $\tau_c[\pi\,\E[\Delta\bar{\theta}_i] + \eta\,\E[\Delta r_i]]$, where $\tau_c$ is the productive-time scaling. The recentered regression recovers $\hat{\zeta} \approx \bar{\tau}_c(\pi + \eta \cdot \partial r / \partial \bar{\theta})$, absorbing the average productive-time scaling into the reduced-form coefficient. So $\hat{\zeta} \cdot \E[\Delta\bar{\theta}_i] \approx \bar{\tau}_c[\pi\,\E[\Delta\bar{\theta}_i] + \eta\,\E[\Delta r_i]]$ when rank changes are approximately linear in peer-mean changes, which holds well in our data. The accounting exercise uses $\hat{\zeta}$ directly without decomposing it into structural parameters.}

The accounting step is separate from the recentering logic. We use $\hat{\zeta}$ to ask how much of the overall ITT can be accounted for by treatment-induced changes in peer composition. The quantity $\hat{\zeta} \cdot \E[\Delta\bar{\theta}_i]$---where $\E[\Delta\bar{\theta}_i]$ is the observed mean treatment-control difference in leave-out peer means---measures the predicted effect of the peer/rank channel alone. Comparing this to the overall ITT reveals whether the peer channel is large enough to matter, and what must be explained by the remaining channels: assignment gains from reduced mismatch, productive-time effects, and any other mechanisms. This requires only one estimated parameter ($\hat{\zeta}$) and one observed quantity ($\E[\Delta\bar{\theta}_i]$), avoiding the identification difficulties described above.

Tables~\ref{tab:suffstat} and~\ref{tab:suffstat_liberia} report the decomposition alongside descriptive treatment-control differences in classroom characteristics---mismatch, class size, within-class dispersion, grade mixing---to characterize what regrouping does to classrooms in each setting. These descriptive shifts are informative about the magnitudes of the reorganization channels even without estimating the structural parameters that govern them.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Treatment Effects on Test Scores}

Table~\ref{tab:itt} presents the main intent-to-treat estimates. In Liberia, tracking reduces endline scores by $0.212$ SD ($p = 0.12$). In Kenya, the effect is essentially zero ($0.034$ SD, $p = 0.55$). The Kenya estimate is precise enough to rule out effects as large as the $0.18$ SD found by \citet{duflo2011peer}; the minimum detectable effect at 80\% power is 0.15 SD. The Liberia estimate is less precise (MDE $= 0.38$ SD), but the point estimate is negative, and the pattern beneath the average is informative.

Table~\ref{tab:upper_lower} breaks the Liberia result by track. Upper-grade students (Grades 3--4) are more negatively affected ($-0.348$ SD, $p = 0.03$) than lower-grade students ($-0.089$ SD, $p = 0.52$), and students assigned to the upper track are harmed by an additional $-0.328$ SD ($p = 0.11$) relative to lower-track students, for a total upper-track effect of $-0.348$ SD ($p = 0.03$). In Kenya, the upper-lower split is uninformative: both track-specific effects are close to zero.

\subsection{Signal Quality, Assignment, and Classroom Reallocation}

Table~\ref{tab:signal_quality} and Figure~\ref{fig:posterior_shrinkage} report baseline predictive power. In Kenya, $R^2_g$ is 0.509 in Grade 1 and 0.559 in Grade 2. In Liberia, the corresponding values are only 0.034--0.065 across grades. Figure~\ref{fig:posterior_shrinkage} makes the consequence visible: in Kenya, the empirical Bayes posterior remains responsive to the raw score, so the diagnostic carries real information beyond grade; in Liberia, the posterior is pulled so strongly toward the grade mean that the score-based assignment barely differs from assigning students by grade alone. Figure~\ref{fig:binscatter} shows the same contrast in raw baseline-endline comovement. This $R^2$-based measure blends test precision, achievement persistence, and construct alignment, but the Kenya-Liberia contrast is robust to alternative measures: Table~\ref{tab:signal_quality_alt} shows that within-grade rank persistence from baseline to endline is much higher in Kenya (0.684) than in Liberia (0.242), and that the incremental predictive power of baseline scores over grade fixed effects is an order of magnitude larger in Kenya (0.522 versus 0.050).

A plausible explanation is that signal quality is not a property of the test instrument alone---it is endogenous to the institutional environment in which the test is administered. In Kenya, Bridge operated its own private-school network, building and running schools under a highly centralized model with control over curriculum, teacher training, and school operations. In Liberia, by contrast, Bridge participated in the Partnership Schools for Liberia reform by managing existing public schools that remained government-owned, free to students, non-selective, and staffed by civil-service teachers on the government payroll. In a weaker institutional environment, the same forces that make instruction less effective---less trained teachers, less structured classrooms, wider heterogeneity in what students have actually been exposed to---also make a one-time diagnostic less predictive. The test is administered under the same constraints that limit learning, and the students it measures have followed less standardized paths to the assessment. Signal quality and system quality are jointly determined, not independent.

Table~\ref{tab:assignment_cutoffs} and Figure~\ref{fig:deterministic_cutoffs} confirm that treated schools in both experiments follow the published cutoffs exactly, with zero observed assignment error. In Liberia, the diagnostic is applied precisely, but to a signal that carries little information.

Table~\ref{tab:classroom_reallocation} and Figure~\ref{fig:classroom_reallocation} show what reassignment does to classrooms. Regrouping lowers track-target mismatch in both settings: the mean mismatch measure falls from 1.35 to 0.49 in Kenya and from 1.52 to 0.82 in Liberia. The classroom consequences, however, diverge. In Kenya, treated reading classes are smaller on average (20.9 to 18.0 students), though they become more mixed-grade. In Liberia, treated reading classes grow substantially (32.2 to 42.2 students) and become equally more mixed-grade. Figure~\ref{fig:dispersion} shows that the compression in within-class baseline-score dispersion is also much stronger in Kenya than in Liberia.

\subsection{Treatment Effect Heterogeneity}

The concentration of harm in the Liberia upper track could reflect misclassification: students with scores just above the cutoff may truly belong in the lower track, and placing them in the upper track hurts them individually. Table~\ref{tab:track_bins} tests this by breaking upper-track students into within-track ability terciles. If misclassification were the mechanism, harm should be concentrated in the bottom tercile---students most likely to truly belong in the lower track. Instead, harm is largest for the middle ($-0.472$ SD, $p = 0.06$) and top ($-0.354$ SD, $p = 0.02$) terciles, while the bottom tercile effect is smaller ($-0.204$ SD, $p = 0.24$). Table~\ref{tab:cutoff_het} confirms the pattern from the other direction: treatment harm \emph{increases} with distance from the assignment cutoff rather than concentrating near it (Figure~\ref{fig:cutoff_het}).

This pattern is more consistent with a deterioration of the upper-track classroom as a whole. Table~\ref{tab:classroom_reallocation} shows that regrouping increases mean reading-class size in Liberia from 32.2 to 42.2 students while increasing mixed-grade exposure. Controlling for class size reduces the upper-track interaction from $-0.328$ ($p = 0.11$) to $-0.237$ SD ($p = 0.19$), so congestion contributes but does not fully account for the harm. The number of comparisons grows as we cut by track, grade block, and distance from cutoff, and the cell sizes shrink accordingly, so these patterns should be read as suggestive.

\subsection{Peer Effects}

In Kenya, accurate sorting compresses within-class achievement dispersion (Figure~\ref{fig:dispersion}) but does not raise mean scores. A natural candidate explanation is that regrouping changes peer composition in ways that offset the targeting gains. We estimate the recentered regression described in Section~\ref{sec:bh} to isolate the effect of peer composition on achievement.

Table~\ref{tab:mechanism} reports the estimates. In Liberia, peer ability has no detectable effect on achievement ($\hat{\zeta} = -0.048$, $p = 0.66$). In Kenya, a one-SD increase in peer ability \emph{reduces} own achievement by $0.134$ SD ($p = 0.02$). Scripted instruction means that instructional level is fixed by track designation, so the decile$\times$treatment$\times$grade fixed effects absorb the mismatch channel; adding classroom-level controls does not attenuate the coefficient. The cross-site pattern is also consistent with the identification strategy: the coefficient is near zero in Liberia, where signal quality is low and treatment-induced variation in peer composition is small, and negative in Kenya, where signal quality is high and peer composition shifts substantially.

The coefficient should be interpreted with two qualifications. First, because peer mean and within-class rank move together in tracked classrooms, $\hat{\zeta}$ captures a composite of peer-level and rank effects (see Section~\ref{sec:accounting}). Second, the Liberia null is also consistent with measurement-error attenuation: given Liberia's low signal quality, peer means are measured far less precisely, and a coefficient of Kenya magnitude would be hard to detect.

\paragraph{Probing peer-level versus rank effects.} The composite $\hat{\zeta}$ captures both a peer-level effect ($\pi$) and a rank effect ($\eta$). Under the model, $\hat{\zeta}(\theta_i) \approx \pi + \eta \cdot (-f(\theta_i))$, where $f(\theta_i)$ is the density of classmates' baseline scores near student $i$'s own score. Students in the tails of the ability distribution have few nearby classmates, so their within-class rank is insensitive to changes in peer composition; students in the middle have many nearby classmates, so rank responds more strongly to peer shifts. If rank effects matter ($\eta \neq 0$), then $\hat{\zeta}$ should be more negative in high-density regions. Table~\ref{tab:density_decomp} tests this by estimating the recentered regression separately for terciles of baseline-score density and by interacting peer ability with standardized density. The tercile estimates are similar ($-0.143$, $-0.116$, $-0.154$), and the interaction coefficient is small and insignificant ($-0.049$, $p = 0.24$). We cannot reject that $\hat{\zeta}$ is constant across the density distribution, so the data do not provide evidence that rank effects contribute to the composite. This null is consistent with a pure peer-level effect, but power is limited: the three terciles each contain roughly 1,500 students across 50 schools, and the within-tercile variation in density is modest.

\subsection{Accounting Decomposition}

We now apply the descriptive accounting exercise described in Section~\ref{sec:accounting}, using $\hat{\zeta}$ and the observed peer shifts to gauge the magnitude of the peer/rank channel relative to the overall ITT. This is not a causal decomposition: $\hat{\zeta}$ is a reduced-form composite of peer-level and rank effects, and the mapping from peer-mean shifts to rank shifts is an approximation. The exercise asks whether the peer/rank channel is quantitatively large enough to plausibly account for part of the Kenya null, not whether it causally explains it.

Table~\ref{tab:suffstat} reports the Kenya results. The peer/rank estimate is $\hat{\zeta} = -0.134$ ($p = 0.02$), and treatment raises mean leave-out peer ability by $0.296$ SD. The implied peer/rank contribution is $-0.134 \times 0.296 = -0.040$ SD (bootstrap SE $= 0.020$). The overall Kenya ITT is $0.034$ SD ($p = 0.55$), so the difference---$0.074$ SD---would need to be explained by assignment gains, productive-time effects, or other mechanisms. The accounting is consistent with a story in which adverse peer/rank effects roughly offset positive gains from other channels, though it does not rule out alternative explanations.

The descriptive classroom shifts in the same table show that treatment does reduce mismatch ($0.427$ SD reduction in absolute deviation from track target) and compresses within-class baseline dispersion ($0.328$ SD), confirming that the sorting rule succeeds on its own terms.

Table~\ref{tab:lesson_completion} provides direct evidence on the productive-time channel. In the model, productive time $\tau_c$ is a multiplicative scaling factor on all instructional channels (equation~\ref{eq:learning}), so the productive-time contribution to the treatment effect is $[\tau_{c(i,T)} - \tau_{c(i,G)}] \cdot A_i(T)$, where $A_i(T)$ collects the within-bracket instructional terms under treatment. Lesson completion---the fraction of scripted lessons delivered---is a natural empirical proxy for $\tau_c$. Treatment increases classroom-level lesson completion by 2.5 percentage points ($p < 0.05$), robust to controlling for teacher attendance and class size, consistent with the Kenya class-size reduction (from 20.9 to 18.0 students). The control-group completion rate is approximately 17\%, so treatment raises productive time by roughly 15\% in proportional terms. If learning gains in developing-country primary schools are on the order of $0.2$--$0.4$ SD per school year \citep[e.g.,][]{muralidharan2019disrupting} and one term generates roughly a third of that, a 15\% proportional increase in $\tau$ implies a productive-time contribution of $0.010$--$0.020$ SD.\footnote{The administrative data record completion of supplementary revision lessons rather than the full scripted curriculum. If the measure captures only this narrow slice of instruction rather than overall classroom productivity, the implied contribution is an order of magnitude smaller ($0.002$--$0.003$ SD). The qualitative interpretation is the same under either reading: the productive-time channel is positive but well below the magnitude of the peer/rank effect.} Even at the upper end of this range, the productive-time contribution is half the magnitude of the peer/rank effect ($-0.040$ SD). All three non-peer channels therefore push positive---mismatch falls, within-class dispersion compresses, lesson completion rises---leaving adverse peer composition as the only negative force and the dominant constraint in the Kenya accounting.

Table~\ref{tab:suffstat_liberia} reports the Liberia results. The recentered peer estimate is near zero ($\hat{\zeta} = -0.048$, $p = 0.66$), and the treatment-induced peer shift is small ($0.041$ SD), so the peer/rank contribution is negligible. The near-zero peer estimate is consistent with low signal quality: treatment-induced peer variation in Liberia is too small for the regression to detect an effect, even if one exists. The overall Liberia ITT of $-0.212$ SD ($p = 0.12$) is therefore more plausibly attributable to the organizational costs of regrouping---class-size increases of roughly 10 students and greater grade mixing---than to peer-composition effects.

The Kenya-Liberia comparison illustrates two different failure modes of cross-grade grouping, but it does not isolate signal quality from other institutional differences---the two settings differ in school governance, teacher selection, class-size constraints, and student composition, all of which could independently affect the returns to regrouping.

\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Specification and Inference}

Table~\ref{tab:spec_robust} reports the ITT estimates under alternative specifications---raw baseline score instead of EB ability as a control, models without any baseline control, wild cluster bootstrap $p$-values, and permutation-based inference with school-level treatment permutation. The point estimates are stable across all specifications; the qualitative conclusions do not depend on the choice of baseline control or inference method.

\subsection{Data-Driven Treatment-Effect Heterogeneity}

The subgroup results above are guided by the model's structure: upper versus lower track, distance from the cutoff, peer-composition channels. A natural concern is that these splits are chosen ex post and may not capture the most policy-relevant dimensions of heterogeneity. We therefore apply the generic machine-learning inference framework of \cite{chernozhukov2025fisher} as a data-driven stress test: does a flexible learner, unconstrained by the model's partition, find that the average effect adequately summarizes the distribution of gains and losses?

We estimate an honest causal forest \citep{wager_estimation_2018} separately for each experiment, using only pre-treatment covariates as predictors: baseline test scores, empirical Bayes ability estimates, grade, track-assignment distance from the cutoff, and---for Kenya---historical achievement records from prior terms. Treatment is randomized at the school level; all inference respects this clustering. Predicted treatment effects are based on out-of-bag estimates to avoid overfitting. We sort students into quintiles of predicted effects and estimate reduced-form effects within each quintile (sorted GATES), then test for overall heterogeneity using the best linear projection (BLP).

Table~\ref{tab:gates} and Figure~\ref{fig:gates} report the results. In Kenya, the spread between the top and bottom quintiles is large ($-0.68$ SD, $p < 0.01$): the quintile predicted to benefit most actually experiences a negative effect ($-0.33$ SD, $p < 0.01$), while the quintile predicted to benefit least gains $0.35$ SD ($p < 0.05$). The BLP test does not reject constant effects at conventional levels ($p = 0.18$), so the heterogeneity is concentrated in the tails rather than varying smoothly across the distribution. Two features of this pattern are noteworthy. First, the reversal---students predicted to gain the most are harmed, while those predicted to gain least benefit---indicates that pre-treatment observables contain enough information to detect that offsetting tails exist, but not enough to rank students reliably into a monotone ordering of gains. The forest identifies instability in treatment-effect heterogeneity rather than delivering a usable targeting rule, which is itself informative in a paper about assignment under noisy signals. Second, the large tail spread beneath a near-zero average is consistent with the accounting decomposition's finding that targeting gains and peer-composition costs roughly offset: different students land on different sides of this trade-off, and the aggregate cancellation masks substantial individual-level variation.

In Liberia, the BLP test rejects constant effects ($p=0.04$), but the quintile-level estimates are imprecise and the tail contrast is not significant ($-0.35$ SD, $p = 0.23$). This combination---statistically detectable heterogeneity that a five-bin display cannot sharply resolve---is consistent with treatment effects that vary along a few discrete institutional margins (grade, distance from cutoff) rather than smoothly across individual-level observables. The forest identifies grade and distance from cutoff as the most important splitting variables, echoing the model-guided finding that harm concentrates in the upper track of higher grades. The imprecision is itself informative: with low baseline signal quality, even a flexible learner cannot sharply localize who gains and who loses, reinforcing the finding that Liberia's diagnostic does not contain enough information to support fine-grained assignment decisions.

Tracking changes class size in opposite directions across the two experiments: treated classrooms shrink in Kenya (from 20.9 to 18.0 students) and grow substantially in Liberia (from 32.2 to 42.2). The Liberia increase reflects the mechanics of combining two grades' worth of students into two tracks within existing school infrastructure. This raises the concern that the Liberia results are driven by overcrowding rather than by the sorting mechanism the model emphasizes. Table~\ref{tab:classsize_control} reports the track-interaction specification with and without class-size controls. The total upper-track effect in Liberia is essentially unchanged after adjustment ($-0.35$ SD, $p = 0.03$, in both specifications), and the class-size coefficient itself is small ($p = 0.74$). In Kenya, adding class size has no effect on any coefficient. Class-size differences do not drive the treatment effect estimates in either experiment.

\subsection{Measurement and Distributional Concerns}

Two features of the Kenya test could generate a spurious null. First, the composite score has a 50-point maximum, and the upper tail of both baseline and endline distributions reaches that ceiling. If treatment genuinely raises achievement for high-ability students but their gains are censored at the maximum, the measured ITT would be attenuated toward zero. This concern cuts against our interpretation: ceiling effects would bias toward finding a null even if tracking helps, so they make the Kenya zero conservative rather than artifactual. They would also work against finding upper-track harm, which we do not find in Kenya. Table~\ref{tab:ceiling} confirms that the point estimate is unchanged when estimated by Tobit with an upper limit at 50 points ($0.041$ SD) or by OLS after dropping the top baseline-score decile ($0.027$ SD); 176 endline observations reach the ceiling.

Second, tracking mechanically reshuffles students across classrooms, which could compress or expand the overall distribution of scores even if the mean is unchanged. If treatment compresses the distribution---pulling high scores down and low scores up---a near-zero average effect might mask real changes that cancel out across the ability distribution. Table~\ref{tab:score_variance} shows that this does not occur: tracking significantly reduces \emph{within}-class variance ($-0.238$ SD$^2$, $p<0.01$) and increases \emph{between}-class variance by almost exactly the same amount ($0.224$ SD$^2$, $p<0.01$), leaving the aggregate variance unchanged. The sorting rule redistributes achievement variance across classrooms---precisely what tracking is designed to do---but does not compress or expand the total distribution. The Kenya null is not an artifact of distributional reshuffling.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

The general problem this paper addresses extends beyond ability grouping. Many policy settings involve replacing a coarse but established assignment rule with a finer signal: algorithmic risk scores replace judicial discretion, standardized assessments replace teacher judgment, credit models replace relationship banking. The appeal is always the same---the new signal contains more information about the relevant type. Our experiments show that the information content of the new signal is only half the question. The other half is what happens to the production environment when you act on it.

In our setting, grade is the incumbent rule. It is coarse on measured achievement, but it bundles students by age, developmental stage, curricular exposure, and social cohort. A baseline assessment captures current performance more precisely, but acting on it unbundles these dimensions: it mixes age groups, disrupts peer composition, changes class sizes, and reorganizes the classroom as a production unit. In Liberia, where the baseline explains less than 7\% of endline variation, this unbundling yields almost no informational gain while substantially worsening the classroom environment. In Kenya, where the baseline explains over 50\%, regrouping does improve instructional match---yet mean achievement still does not rise, because the classroom costs of acting on the signal are large enough to offset the targeting gains.

This tension between informational precision and organizational disruption is not unique to schools. Any assignment system that replaces an incumbent rule with a finer one faces a version of it. The incumbent rule, however crude, is typically embedded in an organizational equilibrium: teachers know how to manage age-homogeneous classrooms, hospitals are staffed for standard patient flows, insurance pools reflect established risk-sharing arrangements. A signal that improves classification on one dimension may still worsen outcomes if it disrupts the organizational structure that made the old rule functional. The economics of finer sorting therefore cannot be evaluated from the signal alone. They depend jointly on how much the signal improves on the incumbent rule and what the act of reorganizing around it does to the production process.

The evidentiary basis differs across claims. The average treatment effects are experimental, identified by school-level randomization. The diagnostic-quality contrast and the finding that Liberia's sorting fails mechanically are direct empirical observations requiring no structural assumptions. The peer-effects estimates in Kenya are suggestive---they exploit within-school variation under maintained assumptions that we probe but cannot definitively verify. The accounting decomposition provides a qualitative organizing lens rather than a precise quantitative test.

The data-driven heterogeneity analysis reinforces this reading. A causal forest uncovers suggestive evidence of large offsetting tails in Kenya---students predicted to benefit most are harmed, while those predicted to benefit least gain---but does not deliver a stable ranking of who gains and who loses. Pre-treatment observables detect that heterogeneity exists but cannot resolve it into a monotone targeting rule. In Liberia, the forest detects heterogeneity along a few institutional margins but cannot sharply localize individual-level effects. In both settings, average effects mask complex distributions of gains and losses that resist simple characterization.

The practical implication for education systems is that the bar for adopting ability grouping should include not only evidence that a diagnostic predicts achievement, but evidence that the diagnostic improves on grade by enough to survive classroom reorganization. Neither of our experiments clears that bar. In Liberia, the signal is too weak for the question to be close. In Kenya, the signal is strong and the answer is still ambiguous.

Moreover, signal quality is not an exogenous parameter that implementers can freely choose. It is endogenous to the institutional environment: the same conditions that generate wide within-grade dispersion---less standardized instruction, less trained teachers, wider heterogeneity in prior exposure---also make a one-time diagnostic less predictive. The demand for ability grouping is therefore highest where its feasibility is lowest. And as Remark~\ref{rem:wg_cg} formalizes, cross-grade grouping faces a structurally harder version of this problem than within-grade tracking: it requires the diagnostic to produce commensurable rankings across grades and imposes organizational costs---grade mixing, class-size reallocation---that within-grade tracking avoids by construction. The positive effects found by \citet{duflo2011peer} for within-grade tracking are consistent with this distinction. The scope for cross-grade grouping to raise achievement is likely narrower, and the more promising path may be interventions that improve instructional targeting within existing classroom structures rather than interventions that reorganize classrooms around a new signal.

More broadly, these findings point to a gap in how targeting interventions are typically evaluated. The standard approach asks whether a signal predicts the outcome of interest. That is necessary but not sufficient. The question is whether the signal improves on the incumbent rule by enough to compensate for the costs of reorganizing the production environment around it. In settings where production is collective---classrooms, teams, hospital wards---acting on a better signal always has organizational consequences, and those consequences are part of the policy's cost. A framework that evaluates signals in isolation will systematically overstate the returns to finer sorting.
