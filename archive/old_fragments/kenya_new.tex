\begin{abstract}\noindent
This study examines the impact of a cross-grade ability grouping intervention for reading on early grade literacy in Kenyan private schools. Students are tracked by their reading ability, as measured by a pretest assessment, into higher or lower grade reading groups for 40 minutes each week. Estimates of average treatment effects mask important heterogeneity in effects. I estimate a large, positive and statistically significant impact on the top quartile of students. Effects in the middle quartiles are significantly different from those in the top quartile but are not significantly different from zero. I also estimate a large, positive treatment effect on the bottom quartile, although it is imprecisely estimated. I provide indirect evidence that these results are driven by better instructional targeting and direct evidence on the effect of high ability peers.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%Should students be grouped to instruction based on the number of years of education they have received or based on their mastery of material? 
Often in the design of education systems, level of instruction is determined by the quantity of education that a student has received. However, such a classification does not necessarily arise from any theory of human capital but is rather a matter of convenience in modeling education. A more complete model of education would try to account for both the quantity of education or grade structure, given the opportunity cost to every additional unit of education as well as actual learning, as this would be informative about returns to further investments in an individual's human capital  \citep{becker2009human}. Researchers and policymakers have tried to make use students' test scores as an instrument to group students based on prior ability and target instruction more efficiently in classrooms. This is often referred to as `tracking' or `ability grouping' in the economics and education literature. 

The notion of grouping children by past academic performance instead of age or quantity of education has a long history. Grouping children by level of achievement instead of age is an increasingly popular idea both rich and developing countries. Programs grouping children between kindergarten and third grade have been experimented with in schools in the United States in many different forms \citep{edweek20191106}. Cross-grade ability grouping for literacy instruction was one of the forms of tracking that was proposed as a way to improve the efficiency of education in Joplin, Missouri in the mid-20th century \citep{floyd:1954}. The Joplin Plan, as it came to be known, was adopted by several school districts in the 1950s and 60s. \cite{slavin:1987} reviewed evidence on tracking programs, and found that cross-grade ability grouping for reading only was among the most effective tracking interventions. 

While tracking, in theory, sounds very promising, grouping students on the basis of a prior test scores has raised several concerns. First, there may be negative distributional effects of ability leading to educational inequities. Students who are assigned to lower levels of instruction may be harmed either through adverse peer effects or by reduced self-esteem \citep{betts:2011} due to salience of their lower estimated ability. Second, there is a risk of misclassification of students in tracking is based on assessment of prior performance. Test scores tend to be noisy measures of ability, and even a high quality test may not be sufficient to capture the domain that is most relevant to targeting instruction. A test that is a noisy measure of the skills that are needed for students to benefit from a given level of instruction may ultimately lead to ineffective, if not worse, targeted instruction. 

Despite its long history, the evidence around the effectiveness of ability grouping is rather mixed (see \cite{betts:2011} for a comprehensive review). Most of the evidence on within-school tracking comes from high income countries, primarily the United States. While this evidence may provide hints about the impact of tracking policies in low-income settings, among other things, there is much higher variability in student learning compared to high-income countries, impeding the generalisability of these studies to low-income countries. However, some high-quality evidence from developing countries showing that alignment of instruction to students' actual learning level, as opposed to their nominal grade, leads to more rapid learning \citep{banerjee/cole/duflo/linden:2007,duflo2011peer,banerjee2017proof,duflo2020experimental,muralidharan2019disrupting} may suggests that if the distribution of learning levels in adjacent grades overlaps substantially, the effectiveness of schools could be improved by grouping children on the basis of learning level. 

This study examines the impact of a tracked reading group intervention in low-cost private schools operated by NewGlobe Education in Kenya. The program tracked first and second grade students into one of two reading groups based on their performance on English language and literacy assessments. The intervention was specifically motivated by studies showing the benefit of differentiated instruction in schools in low- and middle-income countries. The intervention lasted approximately three months, wherein students met with their reading groups, determined by their performance on the assessment, each day for 90 minutes of reading instruction and practice for approximately 3 months. In control schools, students received the status quo 90 minutes of instruction according to the grade they were enrolled. 

The study a large, positive, and statistically significant impact of tracking on students in the topmost quartile of pretest ability distribution. Effects in the middle quartiles are significantly different from those in the top quartile but are not significantly different from zero. I also estimate a large, positive treatment effect on the bottom quartile, although it is imprecisely estimated. Although the coefficients are imprecisely estimated, the pattern of effect heterogeneity may suggest that students at the top and and the bottom of the pretest ability distribution benefit from tracking, whereas students in the middle quartiles do not experience any sizeable gain from tracking. 

I provide some evidence to show that the impact of tracking is mediated by better instructional targeting for all students, and, for some groups, the presence of high-ability peers. Students in tracked schools experience a statistically significant reduction in within-class dispersion of student achievement, indicating that tracking helped reduce the distance between students and the optimal level of instruction for their needs. I also show that lesson completion rates in schools with tracking is significantly higher compared to treatment schools. 

To estimate the impact of peers, I leverage variation in peer characteristics arising from random assignment of schools to tracking. I exploit the fact that, conditional of the baseline ability distribution in a school, the share of high ability peers in a student's reading group is random. I show that exposure to a higher share of high-ability peers has a large, positive effect on a student achievement. This effect is, however, concentrated in the topmost quartile of student ability. 

The present study makes the following contributions. First, the study provides experimental evidence on the impact of tracking in low-income settings, a literature that is rather sparse, primarily due to the logistical and political infeasibility of random assignment of tracking interventions. Second, our intervention design assauges concerns about educational inequities due to ability grouping common criticism of tracking. Once the reading group session is over, students return to their original grades for other classes. Third, our unique setting allows for greater control over the instructional level as compared to \cite{duflo2011peer}, allowing us to rule out the possibility of no instructional differences in high- and low-track classrooms.

The paper is organised as follows. Section \ref{sec:rev} provides a review of the literature on ability grouping. Section \ref{sec:back} describes the setting of the experiment and section \ref{sec:data} describes the data and summary statistics. Section \ref{sec:empirical} presents the empirical strategy and section \ref{sec:results} discusses the results. Section \ref{sec:conclude} concludes.

The question of whether pupils in a given grade benefit from being tracked into groups based on their initial achievement is a recurring question in the economics of education literature.\footnote{There is also a large body of research on between-school ability grouping, mainly from Europe where tracking between academic and vocational tracks is prevalent. In this paper, we are interested in within-school ability grouping.} Proponents of tracking posit that since it reduces the variation in achievement among pupils, tracking allows teachers to target instruction to the level of the pupils, thus benefiting students in both the higher and the lower tracks. On the other hand, critics of tracking argue that tracking disproportionately hurts the already low-achieving students through negative peer effects as well as loss of self-esteem through knowledge of being placed in the lower track. 

As noted by \cite{betts:2011}, despite its longstanding presence in education settings, the body of evidence regarding the effectiveness of ability grouping demonstrates a heterogeneous array of results. Most of the evidence on within-school tracking comes from high income countries, primarily the United States and hints towards a small to null effect of tracking. \cite{slavin:1987} does not find a statistically significant impact of tracking in his meta-analysis of 14 studies on tracking in elementary schools, of which one had random assignment and three were large-scale, longitudinal studies. At the secondary school level, none of the six randomised studies reviewed in \cite{slavin1990achievement} found a positive effect of tracking, although there was some evidence of a small negative effect \citep{hanushek2006does}. Other recent studies and reviews find that ability grouping has little to no impact on student achievement \citep{kulik1987effects, terrin2022effect}.

Given the nature of tracking, there is likely to be heterogeneity in effects between high and low ability tracks. The overall evidence is inconclusive but usually suggests that tracking may be beneficial for students assigned to the high track and harmful for students assigned to the low track \citep{fu2018ability, lavrijsen2015new, burke2013classroom, hoxby2005taking}. Other studies report large, positive effects of tracking on academic achievement for students in the higher track and no negative impact on lower track students \citep{card2016can, vardardottir2013peer, antonovics/black/cullen/meiselman:2022}. On the other hand, \cite{antecol2016peer} found that tracking benefits the low ability track students and hurts the high achievement class and \cite{garlick} found that tracking (in university dormitories) reduces the GPA of low-scoring students but does not impact high-scoring students. Albeit in the context of higher education, a recent study by \cite{de2022college} employs a regression discontinuity design and reports that students who were marginally admitted to the higher ability class in a public university in Columbia were less likely to pass. 

Well-identified studies on tracking or ability grouping are virtually absent in developing countries.  Among the few, \cite{duflodupaskremer} find positive effects of tracking in schools in Kenya. Students in tracking schools scored 0.18 standard deviations higher than students in non-tracking schools that persisted after the programme ended. The effects were observed on all quantiles of the baseline ability distribution.\footnote{\cite{cummins2017heterogeneous} reanalyzes the data from  \cite{duflodupaskremer} show that there is a positive effect in the low track only when the class is assigned to a contract teacher.} Apart from this, most other studies comprise instructional targeting through various ways in the spirit of Teaching at the Right Level (TaRL) and find significant gains to instructional alignment \citep{banerjee2017proof, duflo2020experimental}. 

Tracking interventions effectively change three components - the level of instruction, the dispersion in classroom achievement as well as the mean classroom achievement.  \cite{duflodupaskremer}, postulate that tracking allows teachers to teach at the level of the students and therefore positively impact both high ability and low ability students. They show that low ability students gain basic knowledge skills while high achieving students gain advanced skills suggesting that tracking allowed teachers to tailor their instruction to the level of the students.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:back}

The Kenyan education system, like in many lower-middle-income countries, offers free primary education, though some costs like uniforms remain. Almost all children attend primary school, concluding with the Kenya Certificate of Primary Education (KCPE) exam which influences secondary school admission. Primary education spans eight grades and the school year is divided into three terms. Pre-primary education comprises three grades for children aged three to five.

Public school teachers, generally more educated than the average worker and represented by a strong union, are government-employed and earn significantly higher salaries compared to those in higher-income countries. They typically hold a two-year post-secondary certificate in primary education. Private education is growing, with about 33\% of pre-primary and 16\% of primary pupils in private schools. These schools, often small and independent, emerged partly due to overcrowding in public schools after the 2003 Free Primary Education Program.

Pre-primary education, traditionally community-run and varying in public or private status, was placed under county government responsibility after the 2010 Kenyan constitution. Despite this, there's still a lack of uniformity in these schools. Teachers often lack sufficient content and teaching knowledge, with high absentee rates and limited teaching time. Kenyan primary teachers, while better than those in some Sub-Saharan African countries, often struggle with content accuracy and assessment skills.

Kenyan learning outcomes are higher than many Sub-Saharan African countries but lower than high-income countries. For instance, less than half of third graders could solve second-grade level math or read English proficiently. Recently, the Kenyan education system underwent significant changes with the introduction of the Competency Based Curriculum, focusing on diverse skills, learner-centered instruction, and increased parental involvement.

This research was carried out in private primary schools managed by Bridge Kenya, a part of NewGlobe. NewGlobe runs private schools in several countries including India, Kenya, Nigeria, Liberia, and Uganda, and also supports public school systems in Nigeria and Rwanda with materials and training. Bridge Kenya has been in operation since 2009 and managed 250 schools by 2018.

Bridge schools primarily cater to low-income families, located mostly in urban, peri-urban slums, and smaller towns. In 2018, the annual tuition at Bridge was around USD 100, which is comparable to other private schools \citep{gray2022can}.

A key aspect of Bridge's approach is the application of modern management techniques for quality assurance. Teachers use standardized lesson plans developed centrally and delivered via digital tablets. These plans, in line with the national curriculum, are created by a team based in the United States and Kenya and are remotely synchronized to the teachers' tablets.

Teacher guides used by Bridge are comprehensive, covering every aspect of classroom instruction, from introducing new topics to guiding problem-solving and overseeing individual or group work. They instruct teachers to ensure student engagement, lead activities at the front of the class, and provide positive reinforcement, such as classroom cheers. NewGlobe emphasizes strict adherence to these scripts, with teachers trained in using tablet e-readers and monitored by academy managers to ensure compliance. While lesson completion, tracked through tablets, is not tied to performance-based pay, it is crucial for feedback and professional development; non-compliance could lead to dismissal. Despite this structured approach, the guides do allow some teacher discretion, especially in modules for individual student work, where teachers can independently engage with students, backed by training in providing feedback and correct answers.

Students undergo regular assessments in each subject, using standardized tests to track progress. Additionally, Bridge mandates regular parent-teacher meetings, primarily focusing on student performance. Previous experimental results have shown that Bridge schools significantly enhance test scores, cognitive skills, and parental involvement in education \citep{gray2022can}.

In August 2018, Bridge Kenya, operating nearly 300 schools in the country at that time, selected 50 schools to participate in a reading group intervention. This selection represented a significant portion of its operational schools in Kenya. Prior to this assignment, in June 2018, baseline English and literacy tests were administered across 292 schools, encompassing all 50 schools chosen for the treatment group. Figure \ref{fig:consort} presents a consort diagram for the study.
 
In the treatment schools, students in the first and second grades were divided into either Blue or Purple reading groups based on their performance in these tests. In the first grade, students scoring above 40 total points in a combined English and literacy test were placed in the Purple group, while in the second grade, the threshold was 35 points. Figure \ref{fig:boxplt} shows that there is considerable overlap between the ability distribution in the lower and upper grades, which is necessary for this intervention to work effectively. 

Once assigned, students in the treated schools received 90 minutes of daily instruction in their respective reading groups for term 3 of 2018, approximately a three-month period. In contrast, control schools continued their usual practice of 90 minutes of grade-based instruction. The assignment of treatment was stratified at the provincial level, ensuring a balanced and representative distribution across different regions. This intervention spanned from September to December 2018, aligning with the Kenyan academic calendar and marking the conclusion of the school year.

\section{Data and summary statistics}
\label{sec:data}

The data for this study, provided by Bridge, includes the term 2 midterm literacy and English test scores used for student assignment to the treatment groups. Additionally, historical data were available for some students in other academic areas such as mathematics, language, and Kiswahili, offering a broader academic performance perspective.

Beyond test scores, the data encompass information on lesson completion rates, teacher attendance, and the operational duration of the schools. These additional data points provide insights into the teaching environment and educational delivery within the schools.

For the endline evaluation, data were collected through a literacy and English test. This test, administered after the intervention period, aimed to assess the effectiveness of the reading group intervention in improving literacy and English proficiency among the students. The analysis of this endline data forms the basis for evaluating the impact of the reading group intervention on student learning outcomes.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:empirical}

To measure the overall impact of the intervention on test scores, we will run regressions of the form:
\[ y_{ij} = \alpha + \beta T_j + X_{ij} \gamma + \rho_t +\epsilon_{ij}  \]
where $y_{ij}$ is a test score outcome of student $i$ in school $j$ at endline, $T_j \in \{0,1\}$ indicates  treatment assignment of school $j$, and $X_{ij}$ is a vector of individual and school level characteristics, including baseline test score controls. $\rho_t$ is a linear probability control for treatment assignment strata. Unless specified otherwise, standard errors are clustered at the school level. Because assignment to the tracking program was randomized, ordinary least squares (OLS) regression identifies $\beta$. The intervention was conducted for both the first and the second grade, I will also estimate the overall effect of tracking by restricting the data to students enrolled in the lower and upper grade separately.

We employ a linear control of the probability of assignment to treatment instead of using strata fixed effects to effectively increase the degrees of freedom in the regression specifications. Accounting for the treatment assignment probability is sufficient to maintain the assumption of unconfoundedness, as discussed in \cite{abdulkadirouglu2017research}. The results are virtually the same when strata fixed effects are included, although the standard errors are larger.



Given the nature of tracking, there is likely to be heterogeneity by student ability. To estimate heterogeneous effects by student (baseline) ability, I employ the following specification:
    \[ y_{ij} = \alpha + \beta T_{j} + \sum_{q=1}^3 \beta_q T_{j}\times \mathbbm{1}{q} + \sum_{q=1}^3 \lambda_q \mathbbm{1}{q}   + X_{ij} \gamma + \rho_t +\epsilon_{ij} \]
where $\mathbbm{1}{q}$ is an indicator variable denoting the student's baseline ability quartile. The other variables have the same definition as in previous specification.

Our data allows us to estimate the effect of tracking on outcomes related to education delivery within schools. Specifically, we estimate the impact of treatment on lesson completion rates using the following specification:
\[ c_{j} = \alpha + \beta T_j + X_{j} \gamma + \rho_t +\epsilon_{j}  \]
where $c_j$ denotes lesson completion rates in school $j$, $T_j \in \{0,1\}$ indicates  treatment assignment of school $j$, and $X_{ij}$ includes school level characteristics like attendance and class size.

To estimate the impact of peers, I leverage variation in peer characteristics arising from random assignment of schools to tracking. I exploit the fact that, conditional of the baseline ability distribution in a school, the share of high ability peers in a student's reading group is random. I show that exposure to a higher share of high-ability peers has a large, positive effect on a student achievement. I estimate the following model:
 \[ y_{ij} = \alpha + \beta D_i + \gamma\text{E}[D_i|\theta] + \varepsilon_{ij}  \]
where $y_{ij}$ is a test score outcome of student $i$ in school $j$ at endline, $D_i$ denotes a treatment such as the share of $i$’s high ability peers, and $\mathbb{E}[D_i | \theta]$ is the expected value of this treatment for student $i$. $\beta$ gives the effect of exposure to high quality peers. Conditioning on the expected value of treatment eliminates selection bias by controlling for the variation in treatment that is nonrandom \citep{borusyak2020non, hirano2004propensity, rosenbaum1983central}. Even after controlling for the expected treatment, the instrument may pick up the effect of being in the higher reading group as students in higher readings are likely to have higher ability peers by construction. To circumvent this problem, I also control for the expected level of each student in the specification. Further, I interact the instrument with the indicator denoting the student's baseline ability quartile to estimate heterogeneous effects of exposure to high ability peers. 

\section*{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Average treatment effect of tracking}
Our analysis of the impact of tracking on student achievement indicates an effect of 0.024, although this effect is not statistically significant at conventional levels. Disaggregating the data by grade level reveals that the intervention appears slightly more beneficial for lower grade students, with a effect of 0.047; however, this effect, while larger than that of the full sample, is not statistically significant. For upper grade students, the effect of the intervention is 0.003 and not statistically significant. In summary, while the intervention shows a positive direction in terms of effect size, particularly for lower-grade students, the lack of statistical significance suggests caution in interpreting these results (Table \ref{tab:avgfx}). 

\paragraph{Heterogeneous treatment effects by baseline ability}
The analysis presented in Table \ref{tab:hetfx} reveals a distinctive pattern of the educational intervention's efficacy when segmented by baseline academic achievement quartiles. For students within the highest baseline achievement quartile, the intervention yields a robust positive effect. The full sample demonstrates a statistically significant increase in outcomes with an effect of 0.11 at the 1\% significance level. This positive trend is echoed in the lower grade, where the effect is significant at the 10\% level, though it does not reach statistical significance in the upper grade. Conversely, students in the lowest achievement quartile, while showing a positive effect in the full sample and lower grade, do not experience statistically significant gains. The middle quartiles exhibit a mixed and generally non-significant pattern of effects. The upper grade students in the second quartile exhibit a notable negative effect, which is significant at the 5\% level. However, most of the coefficients are small in magnitude and imprecisely estimated.

\paragraph{Effects on within-class dispersion}
Table \ref{tab:dispfx} presents the impact of the intervention on the dispersion of student achievement within classes. The outcome measured is the absolute deviation of each student's standardized test score from the classroom mean. The first panel shows the impact of treatment on dispersion in baseline scores. The intervention appears to have a significant effect on reducing within-class variance in baseline scores, with a effect of -0.266, highly significant at the 1\% level. When disaggregated by grade, both lower and upper grades show a significant decrease in baseline dispersion in scores. Lower-grade experience a reduction of -0.188, significant at the 1\% level. Upper-grade classes see a more pronounced reduction in baseline dispersion of -0.343, also significant at the 1\% level. Note that these effects are mechanical, driven by grouping based on baseline scores, and indicate that the invention did what it was supposed to achieve. We estimate a reduction of 0.137 in dispersion in endline student achievement, significant at the 1\% level. In the lower grades, the treatment leads to a decrease in dispersion, as indicated by a effect of -0.121, significant at the 1\% level. The upper grades also show a significant reduction with an effect of -0.149, significant at the 1\% level.

\paragraph{Effects on lesson completion}
Table \ref{tab:lsncomp} illustrates the influence of a targeted educational intervention on lesson completion rates within schools. The treatment shows a positive and statistically significant impact on the completion of lessons, as evidenced by the coefficients ranging from 0.023 to 0.029 across various model specifications. These results remain robust after accounting for teacher attendance and class size as control variables, suggesting that the intervention's structure and content could be key drivers in ensuring that lessons are completed as planned.

\paragraph{Heterogeneous effects of exposure to high ability peers}
The results presented in Table \ref{tab:peerfx} examines the heterogeneous effects of exposure to high-ability peers across different baseline achievement quartiles within Kenyan private primary schools. The table reveals a complex pattern of peer influence on student achievement, which varies significantly across different ability levels and grades.

For the full sample, the highest baseline achievers in the fourth quartile show a significant positive effect from high-ability peers, with a coefficient of 0.40, significant at the 5\% level. This suggests that students who are already performing well can benefit further when grouped with peers of similar or higher ability. The impact is even more pronounced in the lower grade, with a coefficient of 0.54, indicating a substantial positive influence of high-ability peers on younger students at the top of the ability distribution. In the upper grade, the effect remains positive with a coefficient of 0.46, though it is significant only at the 10\% level.

For the other quartiles, the effects are not statistically significant in the full sample, and the coefficients are much smaller in magnitude, indicating a negligible impact of high-ability peers on these groups. Specifically, the middle quartiles do not seem to experience any clear advantage or disadvantage from the presence of high-ability peers, suggesting that the benefits of such peer effects may be limited to students with higher baseline academic achievements.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discuss}


The results of this study provide a multifaceted view of the impact of tracking and exposure to high-ability peers in educational settings. By examining the effects across different student achievement levels and within various classroom contexts, we gain insights into the nuanced ways that educational interventions and peer dynamics influence student outcomes.

Our results indicate that students at the top of the pretest ability distribution experience significant benefits from being grouped by ability, as shown by their improved test scores. This suggests that tracking, when implemented effectively, has the potential to optimize the learning environment for high-achieving students, possibly by providing more challenging material and allowing for instruction that is paced appropriately for advanced learners.

%However, the gains from tracking were not uniform across all ability levels. The middle quartiles did not show a significant improvement from tracking. This lack of benefit could be due to the curriculum or pedagogy not being sufficiently tailored to the needs of these students, or it could be a result of the middle-achieving students receiving less focused attention compared to their higher-achieving peers.

Interestingly, the study also indicated a positive, albeit imprecisely estimated, effect on the lowest quartile. This raises the possibility that even students with lower baseline achievements can benefit from ability grouping if the instruction is adequately supportive and if they are not negatively impacted by stigmatization or lower expectations.

The reduction in dispersion indicates a convergence in achievement levels within the classroom, potentially due to the effect of the intervention on the alignment of instruction to the needs of the students. The larger effect in the upper grade could suggest that the intervention is particularly effective at equalizing achievement among older students. Moreover, the intervention's positive impact on lesson completion rates may indicate the success of instructional targeting that may have allowed teachers in tracked schools to have higher rates of lesson completion. Thus, tracking seems to facilitate a system where teachers can more effectively complete planned lessons, indicating an efficient use of classroom time that may contribute to better learning outcomes.

When we look at the differential effect of peers by baseline achievement quartiles, the benefits of having high-ability peers are most clearly observed among the highest achievers. This peer effect could be due to a variety of factors, including motivation from competition, enhanced learning through peer-to-peer interaction, or the emulation of effective study habits. The significant positive impact in the lower grades suggests that early exposure to high-ability peers can be particularly beneficial, perhaps setting a foundation for continued academic success. The absence of significant positive peer effects in the middle and lower quartiles may imply that the presence of high-achieving peers does not universally enhance performance and that the dynamics of peer influence are more complex. 

In summary, the findings of this study have several implications for educational policy and practice. They suggest that ability grouping and the careful structuring of peer groups can be effective strategies for improving student outcomes, particularly for high-achieving students. However, to ensure that all students benefit from such educational interventions, additional measures may need to be taken to support those who do not naturally thrive in these settings.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclude}

In conclusion, our study revisits the debate on the merits of ability grouping in education, particularly within the context of low-income countries. We explore an alternative to the traditional model of education progression, which is typically dictated by the years of schooling, in favor of an approach that emphasizes actual learning level. Our examination of a reading group intervention in Kenyan private schools provides a fresh lens to assess the efficacy of grouping students by ability rather than grade level.

The results underscore the potential advantages of such strategies, particularly for students at the extremes of the ability spectrum. High-achieving students showed marked improvements, suggesting that when instruction is closely aligned with students' learning levels, there is a greater opportunity for academic growth. The less conclusive results for students in the middle ability ranges and the less precise estimates for lower-achieving students call for a more tailored approach, ensuring that the benefits of ability grouping reach all students.

The intervention's ability to reduce within-class variance points towards a more homogenized and potentially more effective instructional environment. Yet, the observed benefits for students with high-ability peers also highlight the complex interplay between peer influences and individual achievement.

While the study is grounded in the Kenyan educational landscape, its implications ripple far beyond, suggesting that ability grouping might be a universally beneficial approach if appropriately implemented. The short duration of the intervention also opens avenues for future research to explore long-term effects and the potential for scalability in similar educational contexts.

Ultimately, this research contributes to the broader discussion on optimizing educational outcomes through innovative structuring of learning environments. It challenges traditional notions of educational progression and supports the ongoing search for more refined and impactful teaching methods. As educational models continue to evolve globally, the findings from this study offer valuable insights for educators and policymakers striving to maximize learning for all students.
