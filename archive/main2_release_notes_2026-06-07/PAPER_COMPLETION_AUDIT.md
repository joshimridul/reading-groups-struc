## Core empirical story in the current Kenya/Liberia manuscript

The manuscript argues that neither cross-grade ability-grouping experiment generates evidence of average achievement gains, and that the diagnostics point to different constraints.

- Liberia: low baseline predictive power means the diagnostic produces limited useful sorting; treatment also increases class size and grade mixing.
- Kenya: baseline predictive power is high and sorting compresses within-class dispersion, but the null average effect remains only partly explained. Approximate peer/rank diagnostics are large enough to matter in accounting terms, while exact-BH specifications do not support a robust causal peer/rank interpretation.

The first part is well supported by the generated tables. The second part has been revised to avoid treating the approximate peer/rank estimate as the preferred causal explanation.
