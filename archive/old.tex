\begin{abstract}
Cross grade ability grouping within schools may provide a way to more directly tailor instruction to a pupil's ability level. This study examines the impact of a cross-grade ability grouping intervention in schools operated by Bridge International Academies in Kenya. The program tracked students in Standards 1 and 2 into one of two English language reading groups based on performance on a baseline test. The unique setting allows for greater control over the instructional level than previous studies. This feature allows for direct evaluation of the effects of mismatched instructional level. Students in the treatment group perform better on later tests of language and literacy skills, but the effects are not statistically significant. Students in the middle of the distribution are harmed by grouping while students at the tails experience large positive effects. The program lifts test scores of students at the tails of the baseline distribution, but students in the middle of the test score distribution do not appear to benefit from the program, presumably because the status quo instructional level is more suited to their needs. In Standard 1, we find no effect of being assigned to the group with higher performing peers and in Standard 2 we find a large negative effect of being assigned to the group with higher peers, suggesting that the cut-off was misaligned for that group.
\end{abstract}

Cross-grade ability grouping. The observation that students may benefit more from instruction that is targeted at their baseline level has motivated tracking and reading group programs in schools, the teaching at the right level (TARL) approach pioneered by Pratham in India, and recent technological developments in adaptive learning. This study examines the impact of a reading group intervention in schools operated by Bridge International Academies in Kenya. The program tracks students in Standards 1 and 2 into one of two reading groups based on performance on literacy and English tests. The unique setting allows for greater control over the instructional level than previous studies. This feature allows for direct evaluation of the effects of mismatched instructional level. 

Grouping children by level of achievement instead of age is an increasingly popular idea both rich and developing countries. Programs grouping children between Kindergarten and third grade are increasingly being experimented with in schools in the United States (\cite{edweek20191106})
While tracking with targeted instruction does appear to lift test scores moderately, the results are mixed. The program lifts test scores of students at the tails of the baseline distribution, but students in the middle of the test score distribution do not appear to benefit from the program, presumably because the status quo instructional level is more suited to their needs. These results seem to strongly confirm standard models of the instructional level targeting in which there are match specific interactions between baseline student ability and classroom instructional level.\footnote{Such as Duflo, Dupas, and Kremer (2011).} As such, these results provide new evidence on the production of classroom effects.

The results suggest that targeted instruction interventions have promise, but that such interventions may have important distributional consequences. Attention must be paid to identifying students in need of remedial education. Teaching at the right level (TARL) interventions may be effective overall, but some students may be hurt if they receive remedial sessions that they do not need, or advanced sessions that they are not prepared for.

\section{Experiment}

In August 2018, Bridge selected 50 academies out of the 300 academies that it was operating in Kenya to receive the reading group intervention.  The 300 academies represented nearly all of the schools currently being operated in Kenya at that time. 

In June 2018, prior to assignment, baseline literacy and English tests were administered in 292 schools, including all 50 schools in the treatment. In treated academies, students in Standards 1 and 2 were assigned to either a Blue or Purple reading group. In treated schools, Standard 1 students were assigned to the Purple group if they scored above 40 total points on a combined test of English and Literacy, whereas Standard 2 students were assigned to the Purple group if they scored above a 35. The test used for assignment was different in each grade and the tests administered at endline are different, so it is not possible to say whether this choice actually led to lower ``ability'' students being assigned to the purple group in STD 2.

After assignment, students in treated schools then received 90 minutes of instruction with their color group each day for Term 3 (approximately 3 months) of 2018. In control schools, students received the status quo: 90 minutes of instruction according to grade.

Treatment assignment was stratified at the province level.

\section{Empirical Strategy}

To measure the overall impact of the intervention on test scores, we will run regressions of the form
\[ y_{ij} = \alpha T_j + X_{ij} \beta + \epsilon_{ij} \]
Where $y_{ij}$ is a test score outcome $T_j \in \{0,1\}$ indicates  treatment assignment of school $j$, and $X_{ij}$ is a vector of individual and school level characteristics, as well as a constant. $X_{ij}$ may include baseline test score controls.

Because the intervention was conducted for both Standards 1 and 2, we will also explore whether the intervention's impact was different in each grade.

To identify the potential differential effects for children assigned to the lower (blue) and upper (purple) group, we also will run specifications of the form

\[ y_{ij} = \alpha T_j + \gamma T_j \times P_{i} + X_{ij} \beta + \epsilon_{ij} \]
Where $P_i$ indicates that a student passed the grade specific threshold for entry into the upper (purple) group. 

\textit{Measuring the Impact of Assignment to Lower and Upper Section}. - The discontinuity in assignment around the threshold for assignment to the upper (purple) group allows for a regression discontinuity design to evaluate the effect of assignment to the upper section for students with baseline academic performance in an area around the cut-off. 

Because the tests and cut-offs used for assignment into the blue and purple groups were different for each grade, this analysis will be conducted separately by grade. Figure \ref{fig:bsbyt1} shows combined endline scores STD 1 students as function of the score used for assignment. Figure \ref{fig:bsbyt2} shows analogous results for STD 2. 

We will start by testing for an effect of assignment into the upper (purple) group using the following form:

\[ y_{i} = \delta P_i + \lambda r_i + \epsilon_{i} \]

We will also report results using polynomial expansions, academy dummy controls. We will also report results where relationship between the assignment score $r_i$ and the endline score $y_i$ are allowed to vary between the lower and upper group.

Panel A of Table \ref{tab:effectsbygroup} compare effects across reading groups. Table suggests that the intervention may be less effective for students in the purple reading group, although in no case can we reject the null hypothesis that the effects are equal. Effects on literacy for lower (blue) group students are large, but still insignificant. This might be taken as evidence that the intervention was an effective remedial program for low-performing students, but that it fais to serve advanced students.

Because many below median students in STD 2 were assigned to the upper (purple) group, scoring above the assignment threshold in STD 2 may be a poor measure of baseline performance as it includes many lower performing students. For this reason, Panel B of Table \ref{tab:effectsbygroup} also includes results from a test of an interaction effect with a dummy indicating that a student was above median within their grade at baseline. This specification paints a slightly different picture. Above median students appear to benefit more than below median students on all outcomes. In the case of the combined outcome, it is possible to reject the null hypothesis of no effect for above median students at the 10 percent level.

The effect on the overall population conceals heterogeneity in effects across students. Following Kremer et al., we hypothesize that in the absence of instruction targeted to the tails of the distribution, that the middle of the distribution may not experience large gains from tracking. 

Testing this hypothesis we identify those students who are in the middle of the test score distribution. We identify the half of students in the middle of the distribution of test scores. Let $M_i$ indicate that a student is in the middle of the distribution. We then estimate

\[ y_{ij} = \alpha T_i + \gamma_2 T_i \times M_i + X_{ij} \beta + \epsilon_{ij} \]
Where $X_{ij}$ is a vector baseline covariates, including $M_i$, and, potentially, baseline test scores.

The results of this test are given in Table \ref{tab:effectsmed}. These results indicate that the small effects reported in Table \ref{tab:overall} are an average of, on the one hand, a larger positive effects for students at the tails of the baseline distribution and, on the other hand, zero (or sometimes negative) effects for students in the middle of the distribution.
